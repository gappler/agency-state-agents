## The model is fine. What you put around it is the work.

### OpenAI made Codex smart enough that the bottleneck moved. Most people haven't noticed where it went.
- **Source:** Nate's Substack
- **Author:** Nate
- **Link:** https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/codex-plugins-bottleneck-moved
- **Why it's relevant:** The Agency State argument said in OpenAI's vocabulary. The model's good enough now; the work isn't there anymore. The bottleneck is the workflow that lives in someone's head and gets reloaded every thread. Nate calls the answer "plugins"; the engagement calls it pipelines and a knowledge base. Same diagnosis, same prescription.

**REACT:**
The most useful sentence in this piece is the one Nate buries: *the workflow lives in your head, and you reload it every thread.* That's the operational sludge nobody is measuring. Every senior strategist running Claude or Codex right now is paying that cost on Tuesday mornings, retyping the same context that was already there on Friday. The 82.7% on Terminal-Bench 2.0 isn't the news. The news is that a stronger model with a vague environment gives you faster wrongness, not more help. The packaging question — what becomes reusable infrastructure, what stays a one-off — is now the strategist's job, not the engineer's. I've been making this case in workflow terms for six months. Nice to see it land on the OpenAI side too.

**SHARE:**
Nate just said the thing I've been arguing in Claude vocabulary, but in OpenAI's: the model is done, the bottleneck moved. It's now whatever you put around the model — the standards, the context, the failure modes, the parts of your workflow nobody has written down. He frames the answer as plugins. I frame it as pipelines and a knowledge base. Either way, the test is the same: which parts of your work are worth packaging into reusable infrastructure, and which parts are still living in your head? Two years from now, the people who learned to package will be compounding. The rest will still be explaining the workflow on Tuesday morning.

### You gave your AI agent real tools. Here's the 4-part control layer it's missing + the Judge Layer
- **Source:** Nate's Substack
- **Author:** Nate
- **Link:** https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/agent-judge-layer-production-control
- **Why it's relevant:** The architectural version of the same thesis. When an agent moves from suggestion to action — sending the email, updating the record, opening the PR — a better prompt doesn't fix it. You need a separate judge wrapped around the actor. For marketing leaders pointing agents at CRM, ad platforms, or anything customer-facing, this is the layer to take a position on before the first "wait, did it actually send that?" moment.

**REACT:**
The opening sentence is the one to underline: *the next serious agent failure won't look like a jailbreak. It'll look like an email sent because the thread seemed to imply approval.* That's the exact failure mode every operationally honest marketing leader I've talked to is quietly worried about. Approval modals don't fix it — people click through or stop using the system. Telling the model to "be careful" doesn't fix it either. The architectural answer — a separate judge deciding whether each proposed action should move forward — sounds expensive until you watch one quietly-wrong action ship to a customer. If you're running agents anywhere near a live system, this is the layer to have a position on before you're explaining what happened.

**SHARE:**
Production agents are going to start failing in a way that doesn't look like a jailbreak. It'll look like an email sent because the thread implied approval, or a record updated because the old value seemed stale. None of that requires the model to misbehave, which is what makes it hard. Nate's argument for a separate judge layer wrapped around the actor is the most useful frame I've read on what to actually put between your agent and a live system. If you're past the chat-demo stage and the agent can now do things, this is the layer you can't bolt on later.

## AI saturation finally has a number

### How to Stop AI Agents From Frying Your Brain
- **Source:** The Algorithmic Bridge
- **Author:** Alberto Romero
- **Link:** https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/how-to-stop-ai-agents-from-frying
- **Why it's relevant:** BCG and HBR finally put a number on the saturation problem: workers running four or more concurrent agents reported brain fog, decision overload, and intent to quit. Three or fewer reported real gains. For a senior strategist already running three Claude threads while debating whether to add a fourth, this is the data point that names the wall.

**REACT:**
The headline finding is useful: four or more concurrent agents and the gains invert. Brain fog, decision overload, intent to quit. The more interesting finding is the language for the failure: agents drove friction-to-start to zero, but didn't touch friction-to-finish. That's the operational sludge that nobody is measuring on agent rollouts — the half-written drafts, the chats nobody closes, the skills folder that's grown beyond what any one person can hold in working memory. I recognize the description in my own week. Alberto is doing what most "AI productivity" coverage doesn't: naming the failure mode honestly and putting a ceiling on it. Three agents, not seven. The constraint is the working memory of the person running them, not the compute.

**SHARE:**
BCG and HBR ran the survey: four or more concurrent AI agents and workers reported being less productive, not more. Brain fog, decision overload, intent to quit. Three or fewer reported real gains. The headline number is useful, but the diagnosis is sharper: agents brought friction-to-start to zero. They did nothing for friction-to-finish. Worth reading if you're managing a team that's been told to "use more AI" and the output is starting to look strange. The ceiling on this isn't model capability. It's the working memory of the person doing the orchestrating.
