## It's not the model, it's the harness

### Your company is about to get cheap intelligence. That is not the same as being able to use it.
- **Source:** Nate's Substack
- **Author:** Nate
- **Link:** https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/openai-ipo-own-the-harness
- **Why it's relevant:** The clearest articulation of the methods-vs-tools thesis to come through the feed in weeks — written from the model-cost side. Names the layer Agency State sells into: the context, permissions, review standards, and decision rights around the model.

**REACT:**
This is the argument I keep making, written by someone watching it from the billing dashboard. A founder's model bill drops 97% moving to an open-weight model, and the lesson isn't "models got cheap" — it's that cheap intelligence does nothing for you until the company around it is ready to use it. Nate calls that layer the harness: the context, the permissions, the review standards, the decision rights. The labs can sell you a model. They can't sell you your own operating context, or the judgment that decides when an output is good enough to ship. That's the whole game, and it's exactly the part most "AI strategy" skips, because it's unglamorous and it's yours to build.

**SHARE:**
Everyone's pricing the AI labs as if owning the smartest model means owning the next decade. Nate's piece is the corrective: for most real work, intelligence is already cheap, and the bottleneck has moved to whether your organization can actually use it — the context, review standards, and decision rights around the model. He calls that the harness. Read it if you're tired of "which model" being treated as the hard question when the hard question is what you've built around it.

### Your team is running agents nobody owns. The one-page card and two prompts that fix it.
- **Source:** Nate's Substack
- **Author:** Nate
- **Link:** https://natesnewsletteo. .substack.com/p/ai-agent-ownership
- **Why it's relevant:** The accountability gap that opens when agents start doing real work — and Nate's explicit point that this isn't IT governance: one person has to own each agent. Lands squarely for the buyer who deploys without a platform team.

**REACT:**
The line that sticks: an ownerless agent doesn't fail loudly. It keeps answering from a policy that changed last quarter, keeps turning noisy tickets into clean-looking priorities, and the value drains out while everyone stays busy. Nate calls it the haunted house — rooms full of automated systems still running long after the reason for them left. The fix isn't a committee or "the AI team by default." It's one person close enough to the work to know whether the agent is helping or just producing polished noise. That's the right altitude. The danger of agents isn't that they break — it's that they keep working after they stop being right, and nobody's watching.

**SHARE:**
If you're standing up agents on your team, here's the question to sit with before you add another: who owns this one? Not who built it — who's accountable for whether it's still right. Nate names the failure mode nobody budgets for: agents that keep running long after the reason for them disappeared, producing confident output that changes nothing. His ownership test is a one-page artifact worth stealing. And he's right that this isn't IT governance — a committee can govern the road, but one person still has to own the car.

### Vercel deleted 80% of its agent's tools and the agent got better
- **Source:** Nate's Substack
- **Author:** Nate
- **Link:** https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/ai-agent-maintenance
- **Why it's relevant:** Agent maintenance as the undervalued skill, with a concrete case — Vercel cutting tools and the agent improving. Method-level, not implementation-help; the lesson is in the pattern, not a bug fix.

**REACT:**
Two things worth your time here. First, the headline fact: Vercel cut 80% of its sales agent's tools and the agent got better. More surface area isn't more capability — it's more ways to be wrong, and most agents are carrying tools they never needed. Second, and the part Nate actually cares about: an agent is something you maintain, not something you finish. He learned it on boats in Indonesia, where "probably fine" and fine become different words once you're away from shore. The agents you've already built will keep producing work long after they stop being right. Inspection, replacement, skepticism — that's the unglamorous discipline about to matter most, and almost nobody is budgeting for it.

**SHARE:**
Counterintuitive result worth passing along: Vercel deleted 80% of its sales agent's tools and the thing got better. The lesson isn't "use fewer tools" as a tip — it's that an agent is something you maintain, like a boat or a plane, not something you build once and walk away from. Nate's framing of maintenance as the next valuable AI skill is the right one. If you've got agents running that you set up months ago and haven't looked at since, this is your nudge to go check whether they're still right.

### Your skills are leaving your hands. Don't let a rent-a-brain keep them.
- **Source:** Nate's Substack
- **Author:** Nate
- **Link:** https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/claude-codex-agent-skills
- **Why it's relevant:** Directly on the method-as-deliverable theme — your way of working becoming software, and the question of who owns it once it lives in a harness instead of your head. The portability-of-method argument.

**REACT:**
This is the ownership fight one level up from memory. Your way of working — the standards, the shortcuts, the checks that keep bad work from shipping — is becoming software: prompts, SKILL.md files, runbooks, permission boundaries. Nate's point is that once your method lives outside your hands, it shouldn't belong to whoever shipped the best skill format this quarter. It should be yours: visible, movable, testable, usable wherever you work. Today almost none of that is true — you switch tools and the skill doesn't come with you, so you rebuild it from memory while a teammate improves a different copy nobody can find. That gap is exactly what a real engagement closes. The method is the deliverable, and a deliverable has to be portable or it isn't yours.

**SHARE:**
The thing worth owning in the AI era isn't your data, it's your method — the way you research, draft, review, and catch bad work before it ships. Nate's argument is that your method is now becoming software, and the open question is whether it belongs to you or to whichever tool you happened to build it in. If your best workflow is stranded in a chat history nobody can find, you don't own it. Worth reading for anyone thinking about what actually carries over when they invest in building with AI.

### Agent Loops for PMs: 20+ You Can Run This Week
- **Source:** The Product Compass with Paweł
- **Author:** Paweł Huryn
- **Link:** https://www.productcompass.pm/p/loop-engineering-for-pms
- **Why it's relevant:** List-shaped on the surface, but the structure carries a real pattern: a loop fails not because the agent keeps working but because nobody defined "done." Clears the list-shape bar because the insight, not the catalogue, is the point.

**REACT:**
The title reads like list bait, but the spine of this is one sharp idea: a loop doesn't fail because the agent keeps working — it fails because nobody told it what "done" means. Defining done is acceptance criteria, a success metric, and who signs off. That's not a coding detail; that's the management work, and it's the part every "let the agent run" pitch skips. Huryn also does the unglamorous favor of separating routine (fixed steps), workflow (branches on what it finds), and loop (checks its own work until a condition holds) — three things the "loops are dead / loops are everything" crowd keeps mashing together. The 20-loop library is fine. The definition of "done" is the thing to take to Monday.

**SHARE:**
Skip the part of your brain that flinches at "20+ loops you can run this week" — the real value here is one line: a loop fails not because the agent won't stop, but because nobody defined what "done" means. If you're handing agents work that runs until a condition holds, the condition is the whole job, and it's writing acceptance criteria and deciding who signs off. Huryn's breakdown of routine vs. workflow vs. loop is the clearest I've seen. Useful whether or not you ever touch the loop library.

## What stays human when the machine can read the cookbook

### How to Be Irreplaceable
- **Source:** The Algorithmic Bridge
- **Author:** Alberto Romero
- **Link:** https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/how-to-be-irreplaceable
- **Why it's relevant:** An operationally honest read on the one thing the machine still can't do — the skills you can't fully describe — anchored in a real limit (language as the model's weak point) rather than contrarian comfort. Lands for the strategist deciding what to lean into.

**REACT:**
Romero's knife goes in clean: the person who can explain exactly how he does what he does is the one in danger, because the machine needs only a description. We spent our careers documenting ourselves — measuring, systematizing, making the work legible — and that documentation became the training data for our own replacement. His advice is to lean the other way: the judgment, the taste, the things you can't quite put in a runbook. I don't fully buy the romance — plenty of "indescribable" work turns out to be describable once someone's motivated to try. But the core observation is real and useful: the parts of your work that are easiest to write down are the parts that go first, and that should change what you spend your scarce attention developing.

**SHARE:**
A useful counterweight to the "document everything" reflex. Romero's argument: the more legible your work, the more replaceable it is, because a clean description is exactly what the machine needs. The skills that survive are the ones you can't fully write down — judgment, taste, knowing when something's off. You can argue with the edges of it, but it's a sharper question than the usual "will AI take my job": it's "which parts of my job did I make easy to take?" Worth ten minutes if you're deciding where to put your own development.

### How to Use AI to Make You Better at the Right Things
- **Source:** The Algorithmic Bridge
- **Author:** Alberto Romero
- **Link:** https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/how-to-use-ai-to-make-you-better
- **Why it's relevant:** The honest version of "AI as prosthetic" — closing the gap between what you can reach and what you can grasp, without pretending it makes you a genius. Reflective rather than tactical, but a clearer lens for using AI well than most how-tos.

**REACT:**
The framing I'll keep from this: AI closes the gap between your reach and your grasp — the things you can imagine doing but never had the arms to actually do. Romero quotes Venkatesh Rao calling it a "full-stack-genius-god jinn for $20/month," which is funny and also slightly grim, because Rao admits it makes it hard to think of himself as a writer anymore. That tension is the honest part. AI lets you act on more of your ambitions, and the cost is that the thing you were uniquely good at stops feeling special. For a senior strategist, that's the real question under all the productivity talk: which of your reaches do you want to close, and which were the point of the work? Not a how-to, but a better lens than most how-tos.

**SHARE:**
A more honest take on "AI makes you more capable" than the productivity-guru version. Romero's frame is reach versus grasp — using AI to finally act on the projects your ambition could always see but your time never allowed. The catch he's honest about: when you can do everything passably, the thing you were uniquely good at stops feeling like yours. Worth reading if you want a sharper way to think about what to actually point AI at — not "do more," but "reach the things that were worth reaching."
