# Curation Criteria

What "relevant" means for this curator. Apply via reasoning over titles, summaries, and (where available) full article text. Not keyword matching.

## The audience the curation serves

The Agency State buyer: a senior strategist at an internal agency. Strategic and ownership role over marketing processes they direct or lead. Below the enterprise tier — small-to-mid market organizations. Tech-forward but hasn't made AI operational. Buried in operational sludge that gets in the way of strategic work.

See `../../_shared/about-greg.md` for the full audience picture.

## The bar for inclusion

The curated item should land as ammunition for that reader against inertia. Useful, concrete, operationally honest. Not aspirational, not abstract, not enterprise-tier.

If you can't articulate why a senior strategist tired of pushing a rock would want this in their feed today, it doesn't belong in the top 15.

## Score higher

Articles that:

- Speak directly to **AI applied to marketing operations**, marketing leadership, or marketing process work
- Name **specific tools, methods, or workflow patterns** rather than abstract AI strategy
- Come from authors with **operational credibility** — practitioners, builders, operators, not consultants writing think pieces
- Surface **honest reads** on what AI does and doesn't deliver
- Connect to themes Greg is writing or thinking about: **statelessness, indeterminacy, methods vs. tools, operational pipelines, marketing operations as a discipline**
- Address the senior marketing leader's reality: **recurring operational sludge, planning, content production, performance reading, the gap between AI hype and operational practice**
- Reference Claude or Claude Code specifically, since that's the primary tool Agency State uses
- Cover marketing teams below the enterprise tier — staffing realities, what's actually shipping, what's not working

## Score lower

Articles that:

- Are **general AI strategy or executive-level AI think pieces** (the McKinsey/HBR genre)
- Are **vendor marketing or product launch coverage** with no operational substance
- Use **"AI is transforming marketing" framing** without specifics
- Target **entrepreneurs, agency-builders, or AI-consultancy-builders** rather than in-seat marketing leaders
- Repeat well-known points without new specifics
- Are pure AI research news without operational implications
- Are marketing-as-creative-discipline content (Agency State isn't about creative)
- Cover enterprise AI consulting case studies that don't translate down-tier
- **Fail the pattern-recognition test on practitioner content.** The test: does this post name a pattern the reader would recognize in their own work? Pattern-level posts — about method, frameworks, named patterns, threshold observations — land across the engagement lifecycle, with pre-engagement prospects, current clients, and former clients all seeing their own work in them. Implementation-help posts (bug reports, version regressions, billing surprises, "how do I do X" troubleshooting, vendor complaints) don't carry that recognition; they're only useful to someone sitting with the same specific bug. Same operational credibility on the author side — only one kind generalizes.
- Are **list-shaped content** — X tips, Y commands, Z things to try, N best tools. Even when curated by a credible practitioner. The list format fragments attention rather than building it, competes with depth-of-pattern content, and tends to lose its value within a day. Exception: a list that organizes a real pattern (e.g., "the 5 phases of an AI workflow audit") may pass — the test is whether the structure carries the insight or merely catalogues items.

## Edge cases

- **Vendor announcements** can score if they introduce a capability that meaningfully changes what's operationally possible at the mid-market tier. They score low if they're routine product updates or "10 ways our tool helps marketers" content.
- **Founder/builder posts about AI in their own businesses** can score if the operational specifics translate to in-house marketing leaders. They score low if the implicit audience is other founders.
- **Agency content** about AI workflows can score if it's about the work itself. It scores low if it's about positioning the agency.
- **Critiques of AI hype** score if they're operationally honest. They score low if they're just contrarian without offering a working alternative.
- **Programming-implementation posts** (language ports, framework debates, language-specific bugs, dev-tooling complaints) default to rejecting. They score only if a meta-pattern translates beyond programming work — context engineering, knowledge-base building, agent design, method-as-deliverable. A Bun-to-Rust port can survive because a spec-as-machine-readable-contract translates; pure language/framework/bug content does not.

## What to do with borderline items

If an item is borderline and you have room, include it but flag the rationale clearly in the "Why it's relevant" line. If you don't have room, mention the pattern in the "What's not in the top 15 but worth flagging" section. Don't smuggle borderline items in without explanation.

## Calibration over time

`memory.md` accumulates corrections to this scoring. When Greg flags an article as wrongly included or wrongly excluded, the pattern goes in `memory.md` and applies to subsequent runs.
