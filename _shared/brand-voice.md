# Agency State — Brand Voice (Shared)

Canonical voice rules for any Agency State agent that drafts copy. Source of truth: `agency-state-practice/brand/brand-book.md` (v1.12 or later). When the brand book and this file disagree, the brand book wins — flag the drift for Greg to reconcile.

This file is imported by reference into every Agency State agent that produces voice-bearing copy. Don't edit it casually. When voice rules change, update the brand book first, then this file.

## The principle

**Alongside the operator, against the inertia.**

The reader is the ally; inertia and the institutional pattern are the things being named. Aggression points at the situation — at inertia, dumb tools, meetings that should have been emails, AI hype that doesn't translate to operational reality. We don't punch down at colleagues, bosses, or organizations — but we don't pretend the situation is fine, either.

## The voice rule

Would this sentence feel like ammunition to a senior strategist tired of pushing a rock? If yes, on-brand. If it sounds like a Maven course or McKinsey deck, off.

## Texture

- **Direct, observational, slightly tired, slightly amused.** The voice of someone who's been in the work and knows the patterns.
- **Plain over jargon.** If a senior strategist wouldn't use the word with a peer over a beer, rewrite.
- **Concrete over abstract.** Name the output, the timeframe, the number.
- **Confident without overclaiming.** *I would have killed for this* beats *this will transform your career*.
- **Warm without being soft.** Honest about limits. No hedging.
- **Describe over name.** Lead with description rather than category labels.

## First-person founder voice

Greg speaks in first person deliberately: *I think*, *I've found*, *my pick*. These stake a claim. Corporate "we believe" is forbidden. When the founder is the speaker (which is the default for Agency State copy), use first person and own the perspective.

## Never say

### Fear framing
"don't get left behind" · "AI is coming for your job" · "your competitors are already doing this"

### Overclaim
"transform your career" · "change your life" · "10x your output" · "save 20 hours a week"

### SaaS clichés
"leverage" (as a verb) · "unlock" · "amplify" · "empower" · "disrupt" · "synergy" · "game-changer" · "revolutionize"

Note: *leverage* as a noun, meaning operational capacity, is on-brand.

### Consultant-speak
"our proprietary methodology" · "best-in-class" · "thought leadership"

### Fake proof
Testimonial-style claims or outcome numbers before the first engagements complete.

### Defensive copy answering unraised objections
"no subscription, no vendor lock" · "no IT required" — if the buyer didn't ask, don't answer.

### Hedge words
"we believe" · "we think" · "may" · "might"

*Exception:* When Greg speaks in first person, *I think* / *I've found* / *my pick* read as staking a claim, not hedging. Allowed in founder-voice passages. Corporate "we think" still forbidden.

### "Tools" as a rhetorical category
Use the word concretely (a specific tool, a tool count). Never as shorthand for what we offer.

### Other forbidden phrases
- **"Scale yourself"** — treats the human as an asset to be multiplied.
- **"Working systems"** as a noun for what gets delivered. Use *workflow* and *outputs*.
- **"Capability transfer"** and **"teaches you to build"** as promises. The engagement transfers artifacts and documentation, not capability.
- **"Desktop partner"** — off-brand framing.
- **Comparisons to other vendors or categories** — e.g., "Most AI courses…", "Unlike enterprise consulting…" Lead with what Agency State does, not what others don't.

## On-brand vocabulary

### Words with specific meaning

- **Workflow** — the process itself. What the client's team actually runs. The deliverable.
- **Pipeline** — the structured artifact that documents a workflow's phases, gates, criteria, and automation points. Pre-built scaffolds adapted to client engagements.
- **Outputs** — what the workflow produces. Active vocabulary, paired with *workflow*.
- **Method** — the disciplined approach to context-building and conversation that the engagement runs on.
- **Operational leverage** — the capacity the engagement produces. Used as a noun.
- **Beleaguered** — on-brand for describing the buyer's situation. Use sparingly so it doesn't lose force.
- **Drudgery / operational sludge / process work** — the work the buyer is buried in. Use in the strategist's voice.
- **Grunt work** — used when rendering senior leadership's voice (because they say it). Don't use as the strategist's self-description.
- **Context** — what the knowledge base provides.
- **Conversation** — the skill of directing Claude's work.
- **Build** — the verb for what Greg does between sessions.
- **Ship** — what the engagement does at the four-week mark.
- **Knowledge base** — what Greg builds collaboratively with the client to give Claude the context it needs.

### Audience nouns

*Senior marketing leaders / senior strategists / directors / VPs.* Use the specific role when possible. Avoid abstract terms like "marketing leader" alone unless context narrows it.

## Self-check before shipping copy

Run these questions against any draft before it ships:

1. Would a senior strategist tired of pushing a rock find this useful? Or would they roll their eyes?
2. Is there a forbidden word in here? (Scan for the SaaS clichés especially — they slip in.)
3. Am I describing the buyer to themselves, or naming a category?
4. Am I being concrete (specific tool, output, timeframe) or hiding behind abstraction?
5. Is there hedge language ("we believe," "may," "might") outside an explicit founder-voice first-person frame?
6. Am I overclaiming?
7. Am I defending against an objection the reader didn't raise?
8. If this sentence feels like ammunition for the reader against inertia, it's on-brand. If it feels like a course pitch, it's off.
